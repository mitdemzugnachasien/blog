Traveling to Asia by Train

How Does It Work?

If you had walked up to a Deutsche Bahn ticket counter in 2019 and asked, "How do I get from Hamburg to Indonesia?", the answer would have been surprisingly simple: "Take ICE 1903 from platform 13 to Berlin for €34.99, change there to the overnight train to Moscow, then spend another 144 hours and 50 minutes on the Trans-Siberian Railway to Beijing. From there, catch the connecting train south toward Vietnam—and you're almost there!"

Nowadays, the answer would probably be a ticket to the nearest police station to sober up.
The escalation of global conflicts, culminating in full-scale war, has reduced safe overland rail routes to just a handful of narrow bottlenecks.

That naturally raises the question: is such a journey still possible today?

Is It Still Possible to Reach Indonesia Without Flying?

The classic overland route to Asia was the one described above: taking the Trans-Siberian Railway from Moscow to Beijing before continuing onward to Southeast Asia, South Korea, or Japan.
However, since February 2022, traveling through Russia has carried the very real risk of ending up in the middle of a Ukrainian drone attack. Even if you manage to reach the nearest underground station or parking garage in time, the German Federal Foreign Office continues to warn of arbitrary arrests, particularly for dual nationals [1].
As of 2024, "only" around 30 German citizens (roughly half of them dual nationals) were being held in Russian prisons [2]. Still, the combination of Russian prisons and Ukrainian drones was reason enough for me to rule out traveling through Russia—for now.

Your holiday in Russia could end up looking like this.

The alternative route through Iran doesn't sound particularly appealing either. Besides the possibility of being blown apart—this time by an American or Israeli missile—or being arbitrarily arrested [3], there is also the rather unpleasant possibility of facing the death penalty [4].

That leaves all hope resting on one small and rather inconspicuous country squeezed between Russia and Iran on the shores of the Caspian Sea: Azerbaijan.

Following the Silk Road East

The only remaining option is to travel through Turkey and Georgia, squeeze through the narrow bottleneck of Azerbaijan, and cross the Caspian Sea. From there, the journey continues along the ancient Silk Road through Kazakhstan and Uzbekistan before reaching China and eventually Southeast Asia. It sounds simple enough—but of course it isn't. In March 2020, President Aliyev closed Azerbaijan's land borders [5]. With that, the last realistic overland gateway to Asia disappeared—at least for those unwilling to board an airplane. Does that mean I'm staying home? No.

The Plan

Time for a rough plan: I'm going anyway, even though there's a good chance that I may have to fly certain sections of the route—or, in the worst case, turn back altogether. Once I reach Georgia, I'll head for the Azerbaijani border despite its official closure. Maybe there's a loophole somewhere (spoiler: there wasn't). If I somehow make it into Azerbaijan, I'll try to catch one of the cargo ships that cross the Caspian Sea without a fixed schedule. Depending on their cargo, they occasionally take passengers from Azerbaijan to Kazakhstan. Once I've completed that most difficult part of the journey, the rest will be by train toward China and all the way down to Singapore. From there, it's just a ferry ride to Jakarta—and the overland journey from Germany to Indonesia is complete!

With a printed Google Maps page spread out on the table, the first version of my route begins to take shape:

The first draft of the route

Next came the entry requirements. Using the German Federal Foreign Office website (www.artikel.de), I compiled all visa requirements and travel advisories for every country I planned to pass through. To my surprise, I discovered that I hardly needed to apply for any visas in advance. Cambodia and Indonesia both offer visas on arrival. Azerbaijan is the only exception, as an e-Visa is required before entering the country. Since it can be applied for online, I decided to wait until I reached Turkey, when I would have a better idea of when I was actually going to cross the border.

The following days and weeks were spent getting every vaccination I could possibly need, taking out travel health insurance, buying the necessary equipment, cancelling contracts back home, and—just in case things went badly—drawing up both a living will and a last will and testament. I'll cover all of the preparations in a separate article soon. (www.artikel.de)

Then, after weeks of planning and preparation, the journey to Indonesia finally began. Not from Hamburg Airport—but from platform 8 at Hamburg Central Station, boarding the RE1 service towards Rostock. Stage one of the adventure—the journey to Istanbul aboard the modern-day Orient Express—had begun. (www.artikel.de)

Why It's Worth Traveling to Asia by Train

Much like the famous Christmas Tree Cannon (https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Y5kwnis6nXc), some people inevitably ask: "But... what's the point?" Why would anyone spend four months rattling across half the world by train?

Why not simply board a plane and be lying on a tropical beach just 18 hours later?

Reason #1: Flying Is Boring

Flying certainly has its appeal. Within 24 hours, you can practically teleport yourself to almost anywhere on Earth. But that's also exactly what makes it boring—you do teleport yourself. You board a plane in Hamburg with the same people you'll still be sitting next to when you step off half a day later on the other side of the world. Along the way, apart from takeoff and landing, you might catch a glimpse of a few clouds or mountain peaks. With a bit of bad luck, all you'll see are the passengers on either side of you, and with a bit of good luck, you might exchange a few words with them. The real adventure doesn't begin until you leave the airport.

Train travel is completely different. Here, the journey itself is the adventure. Every train is its own little world—sometimes quiet, sometimes chaotic, sometimes sociable, sometimes strangely distant. One day you're sitting opposite an overworked German businessman who looks at you as if you're personally responsible for the delayed train. The next, a family in Uzbekistan is inviting you to share tea and dinner while the endless steppe rolls past outside the window. One thing never changes: as you squeeze down the narrow aisle with your backpack looking for a seat, you never know who—or what—you'll find sitting opposite you.

And, of course, there's one crucial difference: you don't fly over countries—you travel through them. You meet fellow passengers on trains across Kazakhstan, sample traditional meals in Chinese dining cars, and watch the landscapes of Southeast Asia unfold from your window seat. And if you happen to fall in love with a place, you simply get off the train and continue your journey the next day.

One thing is guaranteed: you'll never be bored.

Reason #2: Train Travel Is Better for the Environment

"97% less CO₂ emissions"—that's the figure Eurostar advertises on its website for choosing the train instead of flying between Paris and London (https://www.eurostar.com/uk-en/sustainability#Carbon-emissions). It's no surprise that more and more people are considering swapping planes for trains. The trend has even given rise to a new word: flight shame. And for good reason—a single long-haul flight can already exceed the annual climate-compatible carbon budget of well under one tonne of CO₂ equivalent per person (https://www.umweltbundesamt.de/service/uba-fragen/wie-hoch-sind-die-treibhausgasemissionen-pro-person).

So does traveling to Asia by train really produce 97% fewer emissions?

Unfortunately, the answer isn't that simple. Emission figures vary considerably depending on the source. Unlike aviation, the carbon footprint of rail travel depends heavily on the electricity mix of the country the train is operating in. Long-distance Deutsche Bahn services, for example, produce around 26 g CO₂e per passenger-kilometre thanks to Germany's relatively high share of renewable electricity, while the UK average is around 31 g CO₂e/pkm. China's rail network is likely to produce higher emissions because of its greater reliance on fossil fuels, and diesel-powered trains can reach values as high as 106 g CO₂e/pkm. For many countries, however, no reliable figures are available at all.

Given the limited data available, it would be misleading to calculate an exact amount of CO₂ saved by taking the train all the way to Asia.

What the available evidence does show, however, is that even under pessimistic assumptions, train travel still comes out ahead environmentally. And as electricity grids around the world continue to shift toward renewable energy, rail travel becomes a little greener every single day.

Reason #3: Train Travel Turns You into a Problem Solver

Anyone who has ever travelled with Deutsche Bahn knows one thing for certain: you probably won't reach your destination without running into a few problems—assuming you get there at all. The same is true, only on a much larger scale, when travelling overland from Germany to Indonesia by train.

A 20-hour cargo ship crossing from Azerbaijan to Kazakhstan can quickly turn into being stranded for several days in the middle of the Caspian Sea. Sometimes the train you were planning to take simply doesn't exist, or you accidentally confuse two cities and end up travelling 947 kilometres in the wrong direction, putting yourself two days behind schedule.

And unless you're a polyglot, Google Translate and a healthy dose of patience are often the only tools you have for communicating with the people around you.

Unexpected surprises are guaranteed.

Over time, though, you become increasingly creative—and surprisingly calm—when dealing with these kinds of problems. Eventually, they stop feeling like problems at all. Instead, they become just another part of the journey.

That confidence carries over into everyday life. If you've managed to travel all the way from Germany to Indonesia by train without getting yourself killed, chances are you'll be able to deal with whatever other challenges life decides to throw at you.

To me, that's what this journey is really about. It's proof that it's worth holding on to ambitious goals—even when they seem impossible at first glance.
